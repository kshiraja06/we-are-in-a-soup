How to Set Up a Community Kitchen

A guide to creating shared spaces for connection, care, and nourishment

A community kitchen is a shared space where people come together to cook, eat, and clean as a group. These kitchens are often run by local councils or charities, but they can exist in many different forms. Each community kitchen responds to its own context and needs, yet they all share a common purpose: creating a space where people can connect through shared experiences and common struggles.

Community kitchens also open up practical possibilities like sharing the cost of ingredients, planning meals together, and preparing nutritious food collectively. Because of this, they often become important gathering spaces, especially for people in precarious or transitional situations.

If you're interested in starting a community kitchen, there are a few key things to think about.

1

Define the Purpose and Make a Manifesto

Begin by clarifying who the kitchen is for and why it exists. Decide what the kitchen aims to do, when it will take place, and what resources are required. Whether the intention is to share cultural food practices, create dialogue, offer care during difficult times, or simply bring people together, having a shared purpose helps shape the experience without over-controlling it.

Make a manifesto. Collectively arrive at the core values and intentions that matter to everyone involved, and hold these values as the foundation of the kitchen.

2

Find the Space

A community kitchen can happen anywhere. Where there is a community, there can be a kitchen. Look for a space that is easy to access and feels informal and welcoming. This could be a shared kitchen, classroom, courtyard, or semi-open area. The space doesn't need to be polished, but it should feel safe and comfortable.

Once a location is chosen, check what equipment and pantry items are available. Practical considerations like good ventilation, enough storage and workspace, seating, and access to plug points (if electrical equipment is used) can make the experience smoother.

3

Invite Participation, Not Performance

Encourage people to contribute in simple, personal ways like by bringing a recipe, an ingredient, or a food-related memory. The focus should be on cooking together rather than showcasing skill or expertise. A community kitchen works best when everyone feels equally involved, not observed.

4

Design the Flow and Groups

Plan small groups that rotate jobs or do everything together. Keep a loose structure: arriving and setting up together, prepping ingredients, cooking, serving, eating together, open conversation, cleaning. Allow flexibility so conversations and interactions can unfold naturally.

Cleaning should also be a shared activity. Set up a simple, efficient system that everyone can follow. This is not a restaurant. Participants are not guests. Provide minimal written instructions if needed, but allow shared responsibility to guide the process.

5

Prioritise Slowness and Presence

Avoid rushing the process. Waiting and slowness should be treated as integral parts of the experience, not as steps to be completed quickly. Incorporate gentle activities during slower moments like listening to music, working with clay, or simply talking and connecting with one another.

6

Establish Gentle Rituals & Hold the Space with Care

Simple actions like serving one another and sitting at the same table help create a sense of equality and shared responsibility. Ensure the environment feels respectful and inclusive. Encourage listening, allow multiple voices, and let people participate at their own comfort level.

7

Document Thoughtfully but Lightly

Collect recipes, pictures and short reflections after the session. Documentation should support memory and continuity without interrupting the gathering or experience itself.

8

Keep It Sustainable

Be mindful of food waste, resource use, and accessibility. Make a compost pit for wet waste and segregate other waste to recycle. Use reusable utensils, share leftovers, and adapt menus to what is locally available.

9

Let the Kitchen Evolve

A community kitchen is not fixed. With each gathering, allow participants to shape future sessions. Keep the kitchen open to public as per convenience. Growth happens through repetition, contribution, and care.

A community kitchen succeeds not by scale or efficiency, but by its ability to create connection, shared ownership, and a sense of belonging.
